1. Why these specific questions
The core goal was to translate abstract psychological constructs into observable, answerable behaviors. Each question was designed to:
Be situational (today-based) rather than abstract
Force a clear cognitive choice, not a vague reflection
Map cleanly to one axis only, avoiding overlap
Axis 1: Locus (Control)
The question:
“When something didn’t go as planned…”
This works because:
It anchors in failure or disruption, where locus is most visible
Options distinguish:
Agency (A, C) → reflection, adaptation
Powerlessness (B, D) → frustration, passivity
This avoids asking directly “Do you feel in control?” which produces biased answers.
Axis 2: Orientation (Contribution vs Entitlement)
The question:
“Which of these best describes a moment from today?”
Key design choices:
Uses behavioral evidence, not self-perception
Includes both:
Active contribution (A, C)
Passive dissatisfaction (B, D)
This exposes entitlement indirectly, since entitlement is rarely self-identified.
Axis 3: Radius (Self → Beyond Self)
The question:
“Who comes to mind?”
This is intentionally:
Perspective-based, not emotional
Expands from:
Self → Team → Others → Customer
We merged “empathy” and “customer” into “beyond self” to reduce complexity while preserving psychological meaning.

2. Branching Design & Trade-offs
Why sequential axes (not mixed)
The tree enforces order:
Locus → Orientation → Radius
Reason:
Locus first: establishes agency baseline
Orientation second: evaluates intent
Radius last: expands perspective
Changing this order would distort interpretation (e.g., empathy without agency is less actionable).
Why 12 leaves instead of 16
A full factorial model gives:
2 × 2 × 4 = 16 states
But this was reduced to:
2 × 2 × 3 = 12 states
Trade-off:
 Simpler, more interpretable outputs
 Avoids redundancy between empathy vs customer
 Slight loss of granularity
Decision: prioritize clarity over completeness
Determinism vs richness
Constraint:
No LLM
No free text
Trade-off:
 Cannot capture nuance dynamically
 Fully auditable, repeatable system
To compensate:
Options are carefully worded to encode nuance
Leaf insights provide interpretive depth

3. Psychological Foundations
This design is grounded in established frameworks:
Locus of Control — Julian Rotter
Internal vs External attribution
Core to how individuals interpret outcomes
Growth Mindset — Carol Dweck
Embedded in internal locus responses
Focus on adaptability and learning
Psychological Entitlement — W. Keith Campbell
Captured through expectation vs contribution framing
Organizational Citizenship Behavior — Dennis Organ
Reflected in “extra effort” and helping behavior
Self-Transcendence — Abraham Maslow
Basis for radius expansion beyond self
Perspective-Taking — C. Daniel Batson
Operationalized in “who comes to mind” question

4. What I would improve with more time
1. Branch-specific questioning
Currently:
Same Q2 and Q3 for all users
Improvement:
Adapt wording based on previous answer
Example:
External locus → “Even if things felt out of control…”
This would make it feel more like a conversation than a survey
2. Temporal tracking
Add:
Daily state logging
Movement across time
This enables:
Behavior trends
Coaching insights
3. Micro-interventions per leaf
Right now:
Each leaf gives 1 prompt
Improve by adding:
2–3 actionable next steps
Difficulty levels (easy → stretch)
4. Scoring layer
Map states to scores:
Internal (+1) vs External (0)
Contribution (+1) vs Entitlement (0)
Radius (0–2 scale)
This enables:
Quantitative dashboards
Team-level aggregation
5. Edge-case refinement
Some states (e.g., “External + Contribution + Beyond Self”) can feel ambiguous.
Improve by:
Adding clarifying sub-questions
Or splitting into more nuanced branches if needed
Final Note
This design intentionally balances:
Psychological rigor
Deterministic structure
User experience simplicity
It behaves less like a survey and more like a guided diagnostic conversation, which is exactly the goal of a PDGMS-style system.
